📖 Lecture #2: Pasteurella haemolytica

Bacteriology | Respiratory Pathogen causing Shipping Fever
🎯 DEFINITION: Pasteurella haemolytica is a Gram-negative, non-motile coccobacillus that is a normal commensal of the upper respiratory tract in ruminants but becomes pathogenic under stress, causing fibrinous bronchopneumonia (shipping fever).

1. MORPHOLOGY AND STAINING

Characteristic Description
Shape Short ovoid rods (coccobacilli)
Gram stain Gram-negative
Special feature Bipolar staining (safety-pin appearance with certain stains like Giemsa or Wayson)
Motility Non-motile (no flagella)
Capsule Some strains are capsulated (virulence factor)
"SAFETY PIN Brucella" → Bipolar staining pattern resembles a safety pin (shared with Yersinia pestis).

2. CULTURAL CHARACTERISTICS

Feature Detail
Oxygen requirement Aerobic and facultatively anaerobic
Growth media Grows on ordinary lab media but enhanced by blood or serum enrichment
Blood agar Non-hemolytic or occasionally alpha-hemolytic
Colony appearance Small, greyish, translucent, convex colonies
MacConkey agar Does NOT grow (fastidious organism)
⚠️ IMPORTANT: Does not grow on MacConkey agar — distinguishes it from many other Gram-negative enterics.

3. EPIDEMIOLOGY AND PATHOGENESIS

Epidemiology

Aspect Details
Reservoir Normal commensal of upper respiratory tract (nasal passages, tonsils) in cattle, sheep, and goats
Transmission Inhalation of infected droplets from carrier animals
Predisposing factors Stress factors: transport (shipping), viral infections (IBH, BVD, IBR), overcrowding, poor ventilation, extreme weather changes, handling, mixing of animals
Susceptible species Cattle (mainly), sheep, goats; occasionally wild ruminants

Pathogenesis

Step Process
Invasion Organism colonizes respiratory epithelium following stress-induced immunosuppression
Toxin production Leukotoxin (exotoxin) destroys alveolar macrophages and neutrophils
Immune evasion Capsule (in some serovars) inhibits phagocytosis
Result Alveolar damage, fibrinous bronchopneumonia, septicemia, and pleurisy
"Shipping fever = Stress + Pasteurella" → Stress from transport allows commensal Pasteurella to become invasive.

4. SYMPTOMATOLOGY

In Case of Pneumonia (Shipping Fever)

Symptom Details
Body temperature High fever (rise in body temperature)
Respiratory rate Rapid respiration
Death timeline Acute cases: animal can die within 12-48 hours
Milder cases Spasmodic coughing, recovery uncommon without aggressive treatment
Lung lesions Fibrinous bronchopneumonia, consolidation, pleurisy, lung abscesses
🦠 VIRULENCE FACTOR: The leukotoxin produced by P. haemolytica is the primary virulence factor that destroys white blood cells, impairing the immune response and allowing severe lung tissue damage.

Additional Signs

  • Anorexia (loss of appetite)
  • Depression, listlessness
  • Nasal discharge (often serous to mucopurulent)
  • Pleuritic pain — grunting when pressure applied to chest
  • Diarrhea may accompany systemic infection

5. DIAGNOSIS

Method Details
Clinical signs Acute respiratory distress following transport or stress
Postmortem Fibrinous pneumonia, lung consolidation, pleural exudate
Bacteriology Isolation from lung tissue, pleural fluid, or nasal swabs on blood agar
Colony ID Small, translucent colonies; Gram stain shows bipolar staining coccobacilli
Biochemical Catalase +, oxidase +, sugar fermentation tests

6. CONTROL AND PREVENTION

Strategy Details
Vaccination Available bacterins and modified live vaccines (often combined with other respiratory pathogens like IBR, PI3, BVD)
Vaccine timing Administered before stress events (transport, weaning, comingling)
Antibiotics Therapeutic use: penicillin, tetracyclines, florfenicol; prophylaxis during high-risk periods
Stress reduction Proper ventilation, avoid overcrowding, gradual weaning, minimize transport duration, provide rest and feeding breaks
Management Separate sick animals; maintain good hygiene; avoid mixing different age groups
✅ KEY PREVENTION POINTS: Combine vaccination with good husbandry practices. Reduce stress before transport; minimize exposure to viral infections; early detection and isolation of affected animals.

7. PUBLIC HEALTH SIGNIFICANCE

Zoonotic potential: Pasteurella haemolytica is generally not considered a significant human pathogen. Rare zoonotic transmission via animal bites or close contact may cause wound infections or respiratory illness in immunocompromised individuals, but this is uncommon.

📖 Lecture #1: Brucellosis

Bacteriology | Zoonotic Disease of Reproductive Tract
🎯 DEFINITION: Brucellosis is a zoonotic bacterial disease caused by genus Brucella, primarily affecting the reproductive system of animals, causing abortion and infertility.

1. CAUSATIVE AGENTS

Species Common Name Primary Hosts
Br. abortus Cattle brucellosis Cattle, buffalo
Br. melitensis Goat/sheep brucellosis Goats, sheep, humans (highly pathogenic)
Br. suis Swine brucellosis Pigs, wild boar
Br. ovis Ovine epididymitis Sheep (rams)
Br. canis Canine brucellosis Dogs
"ABORT-Suis-Ovis-Canis" → Remember all 5 species: abortus, melitensis, suis, ovis, canis.

2. AFFECTED ANIMALS

Primary hosts: Cattle, pigs, goats, sheep. The common manifestation is infection of the reproductive tract and abortion in these animals.

Other animals: May be associated with transmission (wildlife reservoirs, incidental hosts).

⚠️ IMPORTANT: Br. melitensis is the most pathogenic for humans (causes Malta fever, undulant fever).

3. MORPHOLOGY AND STAINING

Characteristic Description
Shape Bacilli or coccobacilli (short rods)
Motility Non-motile (no flagella)
Spore formation Do not form spores
Gram stain Gram-negative
"Brucella = Bad Rods NEGATIVE" → Gram-negative non-motile coccobacilli.

4. CULTURAL CHARACTERISTICS

Feature Detail
Growth time Colonies appear after 48 hours on lab media
Atmosphere requirement 5-10% CO₂ enriched atmosphere (for Br. abortus, Br. ovis)
Colony appearance Smooth, convex, non-pigmented colonies on nutrient agar
Media used Serum dextrose agar (for bacteriological examination)

5. ANTIGENS AND TOXINS

Antigen Type Description
A antigen Smooth-type lipopolysaccharide (LPS) antigen, heat-stable
M antigen Polysaccharide antigen, heat-labile, species-specific

Virulence factors: Brucella does NOT produce classical exotoxins. Virulence is mediated by:

  • Lipopolysaccharide (LPS) - resistant to complement and phagocytosis
  • Ability to survive and replicate inside macrophages
  • Inhibition of phagosome-lysosome fusion

6. EPIDEMIOLOGY AND PATHOGENESIS IN CATTLE

Epidemiology

Aspect Details
Distribution Worldwide occurrence; incidence 1-20% in various regions
Source of infection Aborted fetuses, placenta, genital discharge (main sources)
Transmission routes Ingestion, coitus, artificial insemination, conjunctiva, skin penetration
Age susceptibility Calves relatively insensitive; pregnant animals most susceptible

Pathogenesis

Step Process
Predilection site Gravid uterus and fetus (erythritol-rich environment)
Erythritol Saccharide alcohol in placenta/testicular tissue → stimulates Brucella growth
Result Placentitis → abortion or premature birth
Excretion pattern Intermittent excretion in milk, vaginal discharge, semen for months after parturition
In bulls Testicle, seminal vesicle, epididymis infected → organisms in semen
"ERYTHRITOL ATTRACTION" → Fetus and placenta contain erythritol which attracts and promotes Brucella multiplication.

7. SYMPTOMATOLOGY

In Cattle (Br. abortus)

Symptom Details
Abortion timing At 7th-8th month of pregnancy (or earlier with heavy exposure)
Placenta Often retained at full term
Recurrence Most animals abort only once although infection persists
Herd pattern 30-40% abortion in infected year, decreasing in subsequent years
Introduction risk Bringing in susceptible animals can cause new abortion waves

In Sheep (Br. melitensis)

Symptom Details
Abortion timing At 3rd-4th month of pregnancy (sometimes full term)
Other signs Lameness, mastitis

In Pigs (Br. suis)

Symptom Details
Main sign Abortion (primary symptom)

In Dogs (Br. canis)

Symptom Details
Main sign Early embryonic death or abortion at 5th day of pregnancy
Differentiate abortion timing: Cattle (7-8 months) vs Sheep (3-4 months). This is a classic exam question!

8. DIAGNOSIS

Bacteriological Examination

Method Details
Sample collection Milk, uterine discharge, aborted fetus tissues (liver, spleen, placenta)
Culture media Serum dextrose agar (enriched with 5-10% CO₂, 48h incubation)
Colony appearance Smooth, small, convex, non-pigmented
Identification Biochemical tests (urease positive), serotyping, PCR

Serological Tests

Test Full Name Principle Sample
SAT Serum Agglutination Test Antigen-antibody agglutination Serum
RBPT Rose Bengal Plate Test Rapid slide agglutination Serum (screening)
MRT Milk Ring Test Antibody in milk forms ring Milk (herd screening)
CFT Complement Fixation Test Complement fixation by antigen-antibody complex Serum (confirmatory)
⚠️ BIOHAZARD: Brucella is a Category B bioterrorism agent and highly infectious to laboratory personnel. Handle under BSL-3 conditions.

9. CONTROL AND PREVENTION

Strategy Details
Test and Slaughter Identify and eliminate infected animals (test-positive animals are culled)
Vaccination Br. abortus Strain 19 vaccine (live attenuated) given to heifers before first pregnancy
Vaccination timing One dose administered at 4-8 months of age (before 5th pregnancy)
Management Isolation of aborting animals; proper disposal (burning/burying) of placenta and aborted fetuses
Hygiene Sterilize contaminated materials; disinfect calving areas
AI safety Use Br. abortus-free semen; disinfect equipment

Vaccine Details (Strain 19)

  • Live attenuated vaccine, administered subcutaneously
  • Does not provide complete protection but significantly reduces abortion rates
  • Can cause serological positivity (differentiate infected from vaccinated animals - DIVA strategy)
  • Revaccination may be needed periodically
✅ KEY PREVENTION POINTS: Regular serological screening of herd, culling reactors, vaccination of young stock, and avoiding purchase of animals from infected herds.

10. PUBLIC HEALTH SIGNIFICANCE

Zoonotic alert: Humans acquire infection via:
  • Ingestion of unpasteurized milk, cheese, or undercooked meat
  • Inhalation of aerosols (farmers, veterinarians, lab workers)
  • Direct contact with infected animals or tissues
Human symptoms: Undulant fever ( Malta fever) — recurrent fever, sweats, malaise, arthralgia, orchitis.

📖 Lecture #3: Salmonellosis

Bacteriology | Multi-species Enteric Pathogen
🎯 DEFINITION: Salmonellosis is a bacterial disease caused by Salmonella species, characterized by gastrointestinal disease, septicemia, and abortion in various animal species. It has significant economic impact in both human and veterinary medicine.

1. SALMONELLOSIS IN HORSES

Causative Agents

Species Common Name
Salmonella abortus equi Equine abortion agent
Salmonella typhimurium Enteric/abortion agent

Epidemiology and Pathogenesis

Aspect Details
Source of infection Pasture, food, water contaminated with fetal fluids, fetal membranes from abortions, or infected feces
Susceptibility All age groups and both sexes become infected
Pregnant mares Abort at end of gestation or give birth to dead foal

Symptoms

Symptom Details
Primary sign Abortion in pregnant mares
GI signs Mild diarrhea, slight rise in temperature, inappetence

In Newborn Foals

Symptom Details
Systemic infection Septicemia, weakness, diarrhea, pyrexia (fever)
Respiratory Labored breathing
Outcome Death within 2-3 days
⚠️ NOTE: S. abortus equi is host-adapted to horses and primarily causes abortion. S. typhimurium can cause both enteritis and abortion in horses.

2. SALMONELLOSIS IN CATTLE

Causative Agents

Species Common Name
Salmonella Dublin Cattle-adapted serovar (host-restricted)
Salmonella typhimurium Broad-host-range serovar

Epidemiology and Pathogenesis

Aspect Details
Distribution Worldwide prevalence
Age susceptibility Cattle of all ages affected; calves more susceptible
Carrier state Adults may be symptomless carriers, excreting organisms intermittently in feces
Environmental survival Organisms survive in feces for 2-4 months
Transmission Contaminated pasture, food, water with feces, aborted fetuses, or fetal membranes
Herd pattern Few animals develop clinical signs; remainder become subclinically infected

Predisposing Factors

Factor Explanation
Parasitic infection Gastrointestinal parasites damage intestinal mucosa, facilitating invasion
Poor nutrition Malnutrition weakens immune system
Stress of transport Shipping stress suppresses immunity, allowing opportunistic infection

Symptoms

Symptom Details
Fever Rise in body temperature
Appetite Inappetence (loss of appetite)
Production Sudden drop in milk production
Diarrhea May develop into dysentery (bloody diarrhea)
Abortion Pregnant animals may abort
Condition Emaciation, dehydration, abdominal pain

Diagnosis

Method Details
Clinical signs Fever, diarrhea, dehydration, drop in milk yield
Bacteriological Isolation from feces, blood, or tissues (liver, spleen) on selective media (XLD, Hektoen)
Serology Bacterial agglutination test (BAT) for antibodies
PCR Rapid identification of Salmonella DNA in samples
"DUBLIN = Dairy UC (you see) infection" → S. Dublin is cattle-adapted and causes systemic disease.

3. SALMONELLOSIS IN POULTRY

Pullorum Disease (Bacillary White Diarrhea)

Aspect Details
Causative agent Salmonella pullorum
Distribution Worldwide; economically important in intensive poultry farming
Transmission routes 1. Vertical: Infected ovaries → infected eggs → hatchery spread
2. Dried fluff (spicules) from infected chicks
3. Feces of infected chicks
4. Respiratory route (inhalation)

Symptoms of Pullorum Disease

Age Group Signs
Young chicks Highly susceptible post-hatching; 95% mortality; sleepy, cold, huddle near heat; some show nervous symptoms; vent covered with adherent fecal mass; white diarrhea
Growing chicks Less affected; slow growth rate
Adults Usually no symptoms but lowered egg production
🔴 MORTALITY: Pullorum disease causes up to 95% mortality in young chicks, making it a devastating disease in poultry operations.

Fowl Typhoid (S. gallinarum)

Aspect Details
Causative agent Salmonella gallinarum
Affected birds Growing and adult birds (unlike Pullorum which affects chicks)
Transmission Same cycle as Pullorum: ingestion, conjunctiva as portal of entry
Pattern Acute form: high mortality; Chronic form: lower mortality (~50% in acute cases)
Excretion Intermittent shedding in feces
Survival Organisms survive in litter for months

Symptoms of Fowl Typhoid

Symptom Description
General Listlessness, depression
Diarrhea Greenish diarrhea
Comb/wattles Purple discoloration (cyanosis)

Pullorum vs Fowl Typhoid — Quick Comparison

Feature Pullorum Disease Fowl Typhoid
Agent S. pullorum S. gallinarum
Age affected Young chicks (0-3 weeks) Growing/adult birds
Mortality Up to 95% in chicks High in acute, ~50%
Diarrhea color White Greenish
Adult symptoms None (just low egg production) Listless, purple comb/wattles

4. GENERAL CHARACTERISTICS OF SALMONELLA

Characteristic Details
Gram stain Gram-negative rods
Motility Motile (peritrichous flagella) except S. pullorum and S. gallinarum (non-motile)
Culture media Selective media: XLD, Hektoen enteric agar, Salomonella-Shigella (SS) agar
Colony appearance Non-lactose fermenting (colorless colonies on MacConkey)
Biochemical H2S production (black centers on selective agar), urease negative, citrate positive
Serotyping Kauffman-White scheme: O (somatic) + H (flagellar) antigens
Remember: S. pullorum and S. gallinarum are non-motile; all other Salmonella are motile. Also, H2S production is a key diagnostic feature.

5. CONTROL AND PREVENTION

Strategy Horses Cattle Poultry
Management Remove aborting mares; disinfect environment Cull carriers; improve hygiene; separate sick animals Remove and destroy infected flocks; thorough cleaning and disinfection
Biosecurity Prevent fecal contamination of feed/water Rodent control; prevent cross-contamination Egg sanitation; incubator hygiene; prevent wild bird access
Vaccination Not commonly used Available for S. Dublin Live attenuated vaccines (Feed/water) for S. Pullorum/Gallinarum
Testing Bacteriological culture Serology (BAT), fecal culture Pullorum test (serum agglutination), fecal culture
✅ KEY POINTS: Salmonellosis control relies heavily on good husbandry, biosecurity, and culling carriers. In poultry, test-and-cull programs have successfully eradicated Pullorum disease from many countries.

6. PUBLIC HEALTH SIGNIFICANCE

⚠️ ZOONOTIC ALERT: Many Salmonella serovars (especially S. typhimurium) are significant human pathogens causing foodborne gastroenteritis. Transmission to humans occurs via:
  • Consumption of contaminated poultry, eggs, meat, milk
  • Direct contact with infected animals or contaminated environments
Human symptoms: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea (may be bloody), fever, abdominal cramps. Usually self-limiting (2-7 days) but can be fatal in immunocompromised, infants, elderly.

📖 Lecture #4: Listeriosis (Listeria spp.)

Bacteriology | Zoonotic Pathogen causing Encephalitis and Abortion
🎯 DEFINITION: Listeriosis is a bacterial disease caused by Listeria monocytogenes, a Gram-positive, motile rod that causes encephalitis (circling disease), septicemia, and abortion in animals. It is also an important foodborne zoonosis.

1. CAUSATIVE AGENT

Species Details
Listeria monocytogenes Only pathogenic species in genus Listeria; causes listeriasis ("circling disease")
"LISTERIA = Little STRIAE" → Remember it's a small rod that can form short chains.

2. DISTRIBUTION IN NATURE

Aspect Details
Habitat Worldwide distributed; extensive host range
Reservoirs Soil, water, silage, decaying vegetation; gastrointestinal tract of many animals (as commensal)
Survival Can survive in environment for long periods; grows in refrigeration temperatures (psychrotrophic)
⚠️ IMPORTANT: L. monocytogenes is a major foodborne pathogen in humans (from contaminated dairy, meat, ready-to-eat foods). It is a significant zoonosis.

3. MORPHOLOGY AND STAINING

Characteristic Description
Shape Short rods (bacilli), may appear in pairs or short chains
Gram stain Gram-positive (but may appear Gram-variable or negative in older cultures)
Motility Motile (peritrichous flagella at room temperature; tumbling motility)
Spore formation Non-spore-forming
"Little MOTILE rods" → Listeria is motile (unlike many other Gram-positive cocci/rods).

4. CULTURAL CHARACTERISTICS

Feature Detail
Oxygen requirement Facultative anaerobe; grows well under aerobic conditions
Growth temperature Wide range: 4°C to 45°C (psychrotrophic — grows at refrigeration temps)
Colony appearance Smooth, round, bluish-grey colonies on ordinary media
Blood agar Narrow zone of complete hemolysis (beta-hemolysis)
Media Grows on nutrient agar, blood agar, brain heart infusion agar
❄️ PSYCHROTROPHIC: Ability to grow at 4°C makes Listeria a concern in refrigerated ready-to-eat foods (soft cheeses, deli meats).

5. ANTIGENS AND TOXINS

Component Details
O antigen Somatic (cell wall) antigen; heat-stable; used for serotyping
H antigen Flagellar antigen; heat-labile; phase variation possible

Virulence Factors

  • Listeriolysin O (LLO) — cholesterol-dependent cytolysin that allows escape from phagosome into cytoplasm
  • ActA protein — promotes actin-based motility (cell-to-cell spread without extracellular phase)
  • Internalins — mediate invasion of host cells (cross intestinal/epithelial barriers)
  • Catalase and superoxide dismutase — resist oxidative killing by phagocytes
⚠️ KEY TOXIN: Listeriolysin O (LLO) is the primary virulence factor enabling intracellular survival and spread.

6. EPIDEMIOLOGY AND PATHOGENESIS

Epidemiology

Aspect Details
Hosts Cattle, sheep, goats, buffaloes, horses; occasional cases in pigs, dogs, wildlife
Seasonality Higher incidence in winter months (linked to silage feeding)
Herd pattern Few animals in herd show clinical signs (sporadic disease)
Sources Infected silage (poorly fermented, moldy); cattle ticks; contaminated feed/water
Transmission Ingestion of contaminated material; possibly via skin/ mucous membrane breaches

Pathogenesis

Step Process
Invasion After ingestion, crosses intestinal barrier using internalins
Systemic spread Enters bloodstream → disseminates to target organs
Predilection sites CNS (brain), gravida uterus, liver, spleen
Encephalitis Crosses blood-brain barrier → brain stem encephalitis (circling, cranial nerve deficits)
Abortion Placental infection → necrosis → fetal death and abortion
Hepatic Multifocal hepatic necrosis in septicemic form
"LISTERIA LOVES LIVER & BRAIN" → Predilection sites: liver (hepatic necrosis) and brain (encephalitis, circling).

7. SYMPTOMATOLOGY

Encephalitis Form (Circling Disease)

Symptom Description
Neck stiffness Stiffness of neck muscles
Head tilt Head held to one side (lateral flexion)
Ataxia Incoordination of limbs; circling (usually toward affected side)
Facial paralysis Paralysis of jaw and pharynx muscles
Tongue Protrusion of tongue; difficulty swallowing; excessive salivation
Recumbency Unable to stand; when down, shows spasmodic paddling of hind limbs
🔴 CIRCLING: The characteristic circling to one side (usually toward the side of the brain lesion) is pathognomonic for listerial encephalitis.

Abortion Form

Symptom Details
Timing Abortion occurs at 4-8 months of pregnancy in cattle and sheep
Other signs May be preceded by fever, listlessness; no diarrhea typically
Fetal infection Fetus often septicemic; liver may show microabscesses

Septicemic Form

  • Occurs in newborn kids/lambs/calves
  • Fever, weakness, respiratory distress
  • Multifocal hepatic necrosis
  • High mortality in neonates

8. DIAGNOSIS

Method Details
Clinical signs Circling behavior, cranial nerve deficits, fever — highly suggestive
Histopathology Brain: microabscesses in brain stem, perivascular cuffing, necrosis
Bacteriology Isolation from brain, liver, placenta, fetal tissues on blood agar (beta-hemolytic grey colonies)
Biochemical Catalase +, oxidase -, esculin +, tumbling motility at 22-25°C
Serology Serological examination (ELISA, complement fixation) — but limited value due to environmental exposure
"TUMBLING at room temp" → Listeria shows characteristic tumbling motility at 22-25°C (not at 37°C).

9. CONTROL AND PREVENTION

Strategy Details
Slaughter All clinically affected animals should be slaughtered immediately (no treatment)
Disposal Carcasses, litter, bedding should be buried or burned (not used for fertilizer)
Disinfection Buildings, pens, equipment thoroughly disinfected (use phenolic or iodine disinfectants)
Silage management Avoid silage with pH > 4.5; discard spoiled, moldy, or heating silage
Feed/water Ensure clean water; avoid feeding contaminated pasture or spoiled feed

Antibiotic Therapy (if detected early)

  • High-dose penicillin G (IV/IM) is drug of choice
  • Ampicillin or amoxicillin also effective
  • Combination with gentamicin may be used for severe cases
  • Treatment generally not recommended for food animals due to public health risk and poor prognosis in neurologic cases
✅ KEY PREVENTION: Proper silage making (acidic, anaerobic conditions) is the most important preventive measure. Avoid feeding spoiled silage, especially to pregnant animals.

10. PUBLIC HEALTH SIGNIFICANCE

⚠️ MAJOR ZOONOSIS: Listeria monocytogenes is a serious foodborne pathogen in humans. Transmission occurs via:
  • Consumption of contaminated foods: soft cheeses, unpasteurized milk, deli meats, hot dogs, raw vegetables
  • Can multiply at refrigeration temperatures (4°C) — danger in ready-to-eat foods
Human disease: Listeriosis — septicemia, meningitis, encephalitis, miscarriage. High-risk groups: pregnant women (can cause fetal loss), neonates, elderly, immunocompromised. Mortality up to 20-30% in invasive cases.

📖 Lecture #5: Escherichia coli

Bacteriology | Normal Flora & Pathogenic Strains
🎯 DEFINITION: Escherichia coli is a Gram-negative, facultatively anaerobic rod of the family Enterobacteriaceae. While most strains are harmless commensals of the intestinal tract, pathogenic strains cause a wide range of diseases in animals and humans, including diarrhea, septicemia, and urinary tract infections.

1. GENERAL CHARACTERISTICS

Aspect Details
Family Enterobacteriaceae
Discovery Discovered by Theodor Escherich (1885–86); originally named Bacterium coli
Distribution Normal inhabitant of intestinal tract of warm-blooded animals; can become pathogenic under certain conditions
Related genera Shigella, Salmonella, Klebsiella (other Enterobacteriaceae)
"ESCHERICHIA = Escherich's COLIn" → Named after discoverer Theodor Escherich; commonly found in colon.

2. MORPHOLOGY AND STAINING

Characteristic Description
Shape Short rods (bacilli), may occur singly or in pairs
Gram stain Gram-negative
Spore formation Non-spore-forming
Motility Most strains motile with peritrichous flagella (sluggish motility); some strains non-motile
Capsule Some pathogenic strains develop capsules, producing mucoid colonies

3. CULTURAL CHARACTERISTICS

Feature Detail
Oxygen Aerobic and facultative anaerobic (grows with or without oxygen)
Growth media Grows on ordinary lab media (no blood/serum required)
Optimum temperature 37°C (range: 20–44°C)
Colony appearance Smooth, moist, grey-white colonies on nutrient agar
Eosin Methylene Blue (EMB) Metallic green sheen (characteristic of strong lactose fermenters like E. coli)
MacConkey's Agar Pink colonies (lactose fermenter → acid production changes pH indicator)
🔬 KEY CULTURE ID: Metallic green sheen on EMB is classic for E. coli. Pink on MacConkey confirms lactose fermentation.

4. BIOCHEMICAL REACTIONS

Test Result for E. coli Interpretation
Glucose fermentation Positive (acid + gas) Produces acid and gas
Lactose fermentation Positive (acid + gas) Ferments lactose → pink on MacConkey
Mannitol fermentation Positive
Indole Positive (red ring with Kovac's) Tryptophanase enzyme present
H₂S Negative No black colonies on TSI/TSC
Citrate Negative Cannot use citrate as sole C-source (green on Simmons citrate)
Voges-Proskauer (VP) Negative No acetoin production
Methyl Red (MR) Positive Strong acid from glucose (mixed acid fermentation)
IMViC pattern for E. coli: ++-- (Indole +, MR +, VP -, Citrate -). This distinguishes it from other coliforms like Enterobacter (- - + +).

5. ANTIGENS AND TOXINS

Antigen Type Description
O antigen Somatic (cell wall) antigen; heat-stable polysaccharide-phospholipid-protein complex; 146 different serotypes
H antigen Flagellar antigen; heat-labile protein; 49 serotypes; phase variation possible
K antigen Capsular/envelope antigen; polysaccharide; 91 types; associated with virulence (mucoid colonies)
Fimbrial antigen Fimbriae (pili) — small filaments for adhesion

Pathogenic Toxins

Toxin Function Associated Strains
Enterotoxin Stimulates intestinal secretion → diarrhea ETEC (diarrheagenic)
Shiga-like toxin (Verotoxin) Inhibits protein synthesis; damages vascular endothelium → hemorrhagic colitis, HUS EHEC (e.g., O157:H7)
Colicins Bacteriocins — inhibit other bacteria Many strains

6. PATHOGENICITY AND CLASSIFICATION OF STRAINS

Abbreviation Full Name Key Virulence Factors Disease in Animals
ETEC Enterotoxigenic E. coli Enterotoxins (LT, ST) → fluid secretion Neonatal diarrhea (calves, piglets, lambs)
EPEC Enteropathogenic E. coli Adhesion factors (intimin) → attaching/effacing lesions Infantile diarrhea (especially in humans)
EHEC Enterohemorrhagic E. coli Shiga toxin + attaching/effacing Hemorrhagic colitis, HUS (O157:H7 — zoonotic)
EIEC Enteroinvasive E. coli Invades intestinal mucosa (like Shigella) Dysentery-like illness
UPEC Uropathogenic E. coli Adhesins (P-pili), toxins, iron acquisition Urinary tract infections
⚠️ IMPORTANT: ETEC is the most common cause of diarrhea in newborn calves (colibacillosis). EHEC O157:H7 is a major foodborne zoonosis.

7. E. COLI INFECTIONS IN ANIMALS

In Horses

Aspect Details
Affected group Newborn foals
Disease name "Joint Ill" or "Sleepy Foal Disease" (septicemic form)
Transmission Ingestion of contaminated milk/colostrum or environment
Symptoms Elevated body temperature, diarrhea, weakness, prostration; may proceed to septicemia and death

In Cattle — Colibacillosis in Calves

Aspect Details
Epidemiology High economic impact; diarrheic calves excrete 24× more E. coli than healthy ones
Immunity Colostrum critical — provides passive antibodies; must be fed within first 48 hours of birth
Passive immunity note Heterologous antibodies (non-matching) provide reduced/absent protection
Symptoms — Acute Scouring (profuse diarrhea), weakness, prostration, rapid death
Symptoms — Less acute Calf listless, fails to suck, develops diarrhea
Feces Grayish-white ("White Scour") with unpleasant fetid odor
Other signs Tenderness/swelling of joints (septicemic spread), pneumonia, abdominal pain
🔴 WHITE SCOUR: Grayish-white, fetid-smelling diarrhea in calves is characteristic of colibacillosis.

8. DIAGNOSIS

Method Details
Clinical Diarrhea in neonates (<48 hours), weakness, dehydration, "White Scour" in calves
Bacteriological Isolation of E. coli from organs (liver, spleen, brain in septicemic cases) or intestinal tract/feces
Culture media EMB (metallic green sheen), MacConkey (pink colonies), nutrient agar (smooth moist)
Biochemical confirmation IMViC: ++-- (Indole+, MR+, VP-, Citrate-); lactose fermenter (acid + gas)

9. CONTROL AND PREVENTION

Strategy Details
Colostrum feeding First feeding immediately after birth (within 2 hours); ensure high-quality colostrum with adequate antibody titer
Hygiene Clean, dry bedding; disinfection of calving pens; prevent fecal-oral contamination
Antisera Injection of specific E. coli antisera (results variable, not always reliable)
Antibiotics Oxytetracycline, Streptomycin (but resistance common); use based on sensitivity testing
Vaccination Vaccines available for pregnant dams (to boost colostral antibodies) and calves (oral/routine)
✅ KEY PREVENTION: The single most important factor is timely colostrum feeding within first 2 hours of birth. Combine with good hygiene and vaccination.

10. PUBLIC HEALTH SIGNIFICANCE

⚠️ ZOONOTIC ALERT: Pathogenic E. coli strains are significant human pathogens:
  • EHEC O157:H7 — causes hemorrhagic colitis, hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS); transmitted via contaminated beef (undercooked), raw milk, water, produce
  • ETEC — traveler's diarrhea; contaminated food/water
  • Vancomycin-resistant E. coli (VRE) and extended-spectrum beta-lactamase (ESBL) strains emerging
Human symptoms: watery → bloody diarrhea, abdominal cramps, HUS (renal failure), fever. Severe in children, elderly, immunocompromised.

📖 Lecture #6: Streptococcus spp. (Mastitis, Strangles)

Bacteriology | Gram-Positive Cocci in Chains
🎯 DEFINITION: Streptococcus species are Gram-positive, spherical bacteria that occur in pairs or chains. They are common commensals but include important veterinary pathogens causing mastitis in cattle and strangles in horses.

1. INTRODUCTION

Aspect Details
Morphology Spherical or oval forms (cocci) occurring in pairs or chains
Gram stain Gram-positive
Spore formation Non-spore-forming
Natural habitat Normal flora of skin, mouth, nose, throat, intestinal and genital tracts of animals and humans
Pathogenicity Cause pyogenic infections: bovine mastitis, strangles (horses), and other suppurative infections

Hemolysis Types on Blood Agar

Type Appearance Significance
β-haemolytic Complete hemolysis — clear zone around colony Pathogenic streptococci (Lancefield groups A–V); pyogenic infections
α-haemolytic Partial hemolysis — greenish discoloration (due to hydrogen peroxide) Streptococcus viridans group; oral flora, subacute bacterial endocarditis
γ (non-haemolytic) No hemolysis Many commensals; some opportunists
⚠️ LANCEFIELD GROUPING: β-haemolytic streptococci are classified serologically into groups A–V based on C-carbohydrate (group-specific polysaccharide) antigens in the cell wall. Important veterinary pathogens: Group A (S. pyogenes), Group B (S. agalactiae), Group C (S. dysgalactiae), Group G.

2. DISTRIBUTION IN NATURE

Ubiquitous — found worldwide on skin and mucous membranes of animals and humans: mouth, nose, throat, intestinal tract, and genital tract.

3. MORPHOLOGY AND STAINING

Characteristic Description
Shape Spherical or oval cocci; in pairs (diplococci) or short/long chains
Gram stain Gram-positive (purple)
Spores Non-spore-forming
Capsule Some species have capsules (e.g., Str. bovis group D — mucoid colonies)
Motility Non-motile (rare exceptions)

4. CULTURAL CHARACTERISTICS

Feature Details
Oxygen Aerobes or facultative anaerobes; some strict anaerobes (less important)
Growth media Grow poorly on ordinary nutrient agar; better on enriched media with blood, serum, or fermentable carbohydrates
Temperature Optimum 37°C; some grow at 42°C (e.g., S. agalactiae)
Blood agar Circular, greyish colonies; α or β hemolysis may be observed
MacConkey agar Most do NOT grow; some species (S. faecalis, S. lactis) grow with small colonies after 48 hours
Remember: Streptococcus grows better with enrichment (blood/serum), and β-haemolysis is clear while α-haemolysis is greenish.

5. ANTIGENS AND TOXINS

Lancefield Serogroups

Classified by group-specific C-carbohydrate antigens (Lancefield, 1933): Groups A, B, C, D, E, F, G, H, K, L, M, N, O, P, Q, R, S, T, U, V.

Virulence Factors (Exotoxins & Enzymes)

Toxin/Enzyme Function
O-Streptolysin (oxygen-labile) Antigenic, forms complexes with antibodies; lyses erythrocytes and leukocytes; tissue damage
S-Streptolysin (oxygen-stable) Non-antigenic; also causes cell lysis; associated with anti-inflammatory reaction
Fibrinolysin (Streptokinase) Dissolves fibrin clots; spreads infection; anti-inflammatory effect
Streptodornase DNase — liquefies purulent exudates (pus)
Hyaluronidase "Spreading factor" — breaks down hyaluronic acid in connective tissue, facilitates spread
"S.H.F.H." → Streptolysin, Hyaluronidase, Fibrinolysin, Streptodornase — key streptococcal enzymes.

6. EPIDEMIOLOGY AND PATHOGENESIS

General Epidemiology

Ubiquitous in environment and normal flora. Disease occurs when host defences are breached (trauma, immunosuppression). Similar toxin/enzyme-mediated pathogenesis as Staphylococcus.

Bovine Mastitis

Aspect Details
Major pathogens Streptococcus agalactiae, S. dysgalactiae, S. uberis, S. zooepidemicus (also S. pyogenes)
Reservoirs S. agalactiae & S. uberis: skin (belly, lips), tonsils, rumen, feces
Transmission Suckling calves, contaminated milk, milking utensils, hands (milker), flies
Herd pattern Difficult to eradicate; chronic carrier state in animals

Strangles (Equine Distemper)

Aspect Details
Causative agent Streptococcus equi (Group C, Lancefield)
Affected age Young horses (1–2 years)
Portal of entry Nasal & buccal mucous membranes
Pathogenesis Invades via lymphatics → local lymph node abscesses; septicemic spread → abscesses in lungs, liver, other organs (purpura hemorrhagica)

7. SYMPTOMATOLOGY

Mastitis (by S. agalactiae) — Chronic Form

Feature Details
Progression Chronic inflammation; milk initially contains flakes
Later milk Thick purulent fluid (foul smell), fibrin, blood
Histology Polymorphonuclear leukocytes infiltrate; alveolar proliferation and fibrosis; lumen blocked with cellular debris
Tissue changes Thickened epithelium, granulation tissue
Production Reduced milk yield; may affect one or more quarters

Mastitis (by S. uberis)

Feature Details
Severity Less severe; usually confined to one quarter
Discharge Purulent yellow secretions

Strangles Symptoms

Sign Description
Nasal discharge Mucopurulent (initially serous → purulent)
Temperature Fever (103–105°F), especially early
Respiratory Inflammation of upper respiratory tract; harsh cough
Lymph nodes Abscess formation in submaxillary, retropharyngeal, and other mandibular lymph nodes; may rupture and drain
Complications Bastard strangles (internal abscesses), purpura hemorrhagica (immune-complex vasculitis)
🔴 STRANGLES: Highly contagious equine disease characterized by fever, nasal discharge, and submandibular lymph node abscesses. Caused by S. equi (Group C streptococcus).

8. DIAGNOSIS

Method Details
Hemolysis pattern β-haemolytic (clear zone) or α-haemolytic (greenish) on blood agar
Edwards medium Contains blood, crystal violet, aesculin — selective for Group D streptococci (S. bovis, S. faecalis)
CAMP test S. agalactiae produces arrowhead hemolysis when streaked perpendicular to S. aureus on blood agar (diagnostic for Group B)
Lancefield grouping Serological grouping using group-specific C-carbohydrate antisera
Mastitis diagnosis California Mastitis Test (CMT), milk culture, somatic cell count
Strangles diagnosis Nasal swab/abscess culture, PCR, serology
"CAMP = B for agalactiae" → CAMP test positive = Group B streptococcus = S. agalactiae (mastitis pathogen).

9. CONTROL AND PREVENTION

Strategy Mastitis Control Strangles Control
Antibiotics Penicillins (effective against most streptococci); intramammary infusions, systemic if severe Penicillin G (drug of choice); early treatment critical
Vaccination Vaccines available but limited efficacy due to multiple species/types; strain-specific protection Strangles vaccines (both modified live and killed) available; immunity may be short-lived
Hygiene/Management Milking hygiene (teat disinfection, clean equipment), dry cow therapy, culling chronic cases Isolation of affected horses, quarantine new arrivals, disinfection of equipment, proper disposal of nasal discharge/abscess material
Biosecurity Test-and-cull for S. agalactiae; prevent calf suckling (use bucket feeding) Avoid sharing equipment; limit horse movement during outbreaks

Important Notes

  • Penicillin is the antibiotic of choice for most streptococcal infections (high susceptibility).
  • Vaccines often provide only strain-specific immunity due to antigenic variation among streptococcal species/groupings.
  • For mastitis: dry period treatment, teat sealants, and culling chronically infected cows are key strategies.
✅ KEY POINTS: Streptococcal infections respond well to penicillin but prevention relies on hygiene, management, and species-specific vaccines. Mastitis control requires a comprehensive program including CMT screening.

10. PUBLIC HEALTH SIGNIFICANCE

Zoonotic potential: Some streptococci are zoonotic:
  • Streptococcus equi (strangles) — rarely infects humans (usually via direct contact with infected horses; causes lymphadenitis)
  • S. zooepidemicus — opportunistic human pathogen from horses (wound infections, rarely pneumonia)
  • S. agalactiae — can cause mastitis in dairy workers
  • S. dysgalactiae — isolated from human infections
Generally, animal streptococci are not major human pathogens but can cause occupational infections.

📖 Lecture #7: Staphylococcus aureus

Bacteriology | Gram-Positive Cocci in Clusters
🎯 DEFINITION: Staphylococcus aureus is a Gram-positive, non-motile coccus that occurs in grape-like clusters. It is a major pathogen of animals and humans, producing a wide array of toxins and enzymes, and is a leading cause of mastitis in dairy animals.

1. GENERAL CHARACTERISTICS

Aspect Details
Morphology Spherical cocci arranged in irregular grape-like clusters
Gram stain Gram-positive (purple)
Motility Non-motile
Spore formation Non-spore-forming
Oxygen Aerobes or facultative anaerobes

Pathogenic vs Non-pathogenic Types

Type Key Test Example Significance
Pathogenic Coagulase-positive Staphylococcus aureus (pyogenes) Produces pus; causes mastitis, wound infections, toxemia
Non-pathogenic Coagulase-negative Staphylococcus epidermidis Normal skin flora; opportunistic in immunocompromised
⚠️ COAGULASE TEST: The key test to differentiate S. aureus (pathogenic, coagulase+) from other staphylococci. Coagulase clots blood plasma — protective mechanism.

2. DISTRIBUTION IN NATURE

Reservoir Details
Animals & Humans Skin, anterior nares (nostrils), saliva, intestines, feces
Environment Water, soil, air, fomites (milking equipment, towels)
Persistence Can survive on surfaces for weeks to months
"STAPHYLO = grape clusters" → Greek "staphyle" = grape; cocci in bunches like grapes.

3. MORPHOLOGY AND STAINING

Characteristic Description
Shape Spherical cocci (0.5–1.5 μm diameter)
Arrangement Irregular clusters (like grapes), sometimes pairs or short chains
Gram stain Gram-positive (purple)
Spore formation Non-spore-forming
Capsule Some strains have capsules (virulence factor)

4. CULTURAL CHARACTERISTICS

Feature Details
Oxygen Aerobic or facultative anaerobic
Media Grows on ordinary nutrient agar at 37°C; best growth aerobically
Colony appearance Circular, convex, glistening, smooth; white to lemon-yellow pigment
Hemolysis On blood agar: β-hemolysis (clear zone) around colonies due to hemolysins
MacConkey agar Colonies pinkish-yellow to deep red/purple (some strains grow, unlike most streptococci)
Other tests Gelatin liquefaction (+); grows in high NaCl (6.5–10%); ferments glucose, lactose, maltose, sucrose, mannitol with acid production (NO gas)
Biochemical Methyl Red (+), VP (variable), Indole (−), H₂S (−)
🔬 PIGMENT NOTE: Pigmentation (white vs lemon-yellow) is NOT reliable for pathogenicity. Both coagulase-positive and negative strains can be pigmented or non-pigmented.

5. ANTIGENS AND TOXINS

Antigens vs Toxins — Definitions

Antigens Toxins
Stimulate immune response Cause tissue damage/disease
Proteins/polysaccharides/lipids Proteins (exotoxins) or LPS (endotoxins)
Not directly harmful Directly harmful
Highly specific Exotoxins: specific; Endotoxins: non-specific
Lead to antitoxin production Endotoxins: no vaccine production

Exotoxins vs Endotoxins

Feature Exotoxins Endotoxins
Source Secreted proteins from living bacteria LPS from G-ve bacterial cell wall
Produced by G+ve and some G-ve bacteria Only G-ve bacteria
Release Released during growth (active secretion) Released upon cell lysis
Potency Highly potent, specific effects Less potent, general effects (fever, shock)
Examples Neurotoxins, enterotoxins, hemolysins E. coli LPS, Salmonella LPS
Vaccine/antitoxin Yes — form toxoid vaccines, antitoxin therapy No — not antigenic in same way

Enzymes & Toxins of Staphylococcus aureus

Toxin/Enzyme Function & Significance
Coagulase Clots plasma → fibrin shield around bacteria; protects from phagocytosis and serum killing; diagnostic test (coagulase+)
Hyaluronidase "Spreading factor" — breaks down hyaluronic acid in connective tissue → facilitates spread through tissues
Phosphatase Splits phenolphthalein diphosphate → pink color (positive test); marker for pathogenic strains
Fibrinolysin Dissolves fibrin clots; may help spread; anti-inflammatory effect
Haemolysin Lyses red blood cells (α, β, γ types); tissue destruction
Necrotoxin Causes tissue necrosis (death)
Lethal toxin Systemic toxin causing shock and death
Leukocidin Destroys white blood cells (leukocytes); evades host immune response
Enterotoxin Heat-stable; causes food poisoning/gastroenteritis in humans; vomiting, diarrhea
"CHaP FuLL o' LeN" → Coagulase, Hyaluronidase, Phosphatase, Fibrinolysin, Leukocidin, Haemolysin, Necrotoxin, Lethal toxin, (Enterotoxin). Major S. aureus virulence factors.

6. EPIDEMIOLOGY AND PATHOGENESIS

Pathogenesis Overview

S. aureus uses multiple toxins and enzymes to invade, colonize, and damage host tissues:

  1. Adherence: Surface proteins bind to host extracellular matrix proteins (fibronectin, collagen)
  2. Evasion: Capsule and Protein A (binds Fc of IgG) inhibit phagocytosis; coagulase forms fibrin clot protection
  3. Invasion & Spread: Hyaluronidase breaks down connective tissue; proteases damage cells
  4. Tissue Damage: Hemolysins, leukocidin, necrotoxins, lethal toxin destroy cells and cause necrosis
  5. Persistence: Survives intracellularly within phagocytes for long periods, forming reservoirs

Bovine Mastitis Epidemiology

Aspect Details
Hosts Cattle, sheep, goats; occasionally other species
Reservoirs Skin of udder, teats; nasal passages; feces; environment (bedding, soil)
Transmission route Via teat canal from contaminated skin, environment, milking equipment, hands of milker
Carrier state Subclinical mastitis — organism secreted in milk from apparently normal udder
Survival Can persist in environment for weeks; grows in NaCl-containing media
⚠️ MASTITIS PATHOGEN: S. aureus is one of the most common causes of bovine mastitis worldwide. It colonizes teat ends, forms biofilms, and is difficult to eradicate.

Other Diseases in Animals

  • Tick pyaemia in lambs: Septicemic infection following tick bites; abscesses in multiple organs
  • Yolk-sac infection & arthritis in poultry: Systemic infection in chicks
  • Suppurative infections: Abscesses, wound infections, pyoderma in various species
  • Metritis, metrophlebitis: Uterine infections post-parturition

7. SYMPTOMATOLOGY — BOVINE MASTITIS

Acute Staphylococcal Mastitis

Symptom/Sign Description
Systemic Sudden rise in body temperature, loss of appetite, depression
Udder Enlargement and hardening (edema, induration) of affected quarter(s); cessation of milk secretion
Milk initially Small quantities of blood-stained fluid
Milk later Thickened, contains pus and shreds of necrotic tissue
Severe cases Affected quarters become dark, cold, gangrenous, may slough; fatal toxaemia within 3–4 days

Chronic Staphylococcal Mastitis

Feature Description
Udder Induration (hardening), fibrosis; chronically damaged
Milk Contains clots, may be watery; cell count elevated
Systemic Animal appears normal (subclinical chronic infection)
Production Permanent reduction in milk yield from affected quarter
🔴 MASTITIS PROGRESSION: Acute: fever, swollen hard udder, blood-tinged milk → purulent. Chronic: hard "lumpy" udder, clots in milk, reduced production. S. aureus mastitis is notoriously difficult to cure.

8. DIAGNOSIS

Laboratory Diagnosis

Method Details
Culture Sheep or rabbit blood agar; incubate at 37°C; examine for β-hemolysis (clear zone)
Microscopy Gram stain: Gram-positive cocci in clusters; no spores
Coagulase test Slide test (bound coagulase — rapid screening) or tube test (free coagulase — confirmatory); clot formation = positive
Selective media Phenolphthalein diphosphate agar: phosphatase + strains turn pink (pathogens); Tellurite agar: inhibits non-pathogens, allows pathogenic growth

Mastitis-Specific Tests

  • California Mastitis Test (CMT): detergent-based test detects somatic cells (indicates inflammation)
  • Somatic cell count (SCC): laboratory quantification of cells in milk
  • Milk culture: quantitative bacteriology to identify S. aureus
Coagulase-positive = Staphylococcus aureus. This is the most important diagnostic test to distinguish pathogenic staph from commensal species.

9. CONTROL AND PREVENTION

Strategy Details
Antibiotics Penicillin preparations (first line), streptomycin, tetracycline, erythromycin, neomycin, novobiocin. Intramammary infusions for clinical cases; systemic for severe. Antibiotic resistance is common — use sensitivity testing.
Vaccination Vaccines and toxoids available (containing inactivated toxins or bacterins); provide partial protection but not complete; often used as part of control program
Milking hygiene Teat disinfection (pre- and post-milking dip), clean milking equipment, proper udder preparation, use of individual towels
Dry cow therapy Antibiotic infusion at end of lactation to clear existing infection and prevent new infections during dry period
Culling Remove chronically infected cows with recurrent mastitis (major reservoir)
Management Clean, dry bedding; prompt treatment of cases; record keeping; prevent overcrowding
✅ KEY PREVENTION: A comprehensive mastitis control program includes: proper milking hygiene + teat dipping + dry cow therapy + culling chronic cases + regular SCC monitoring.

10. PUBLIC HEALTH SIGNIFICANCE

⚠️ ZOONOTIC ALERT: Staphylococcus aureus is a major human pathogen:
  • Food poisoning: Enterotoxin in contaminated milk, meat, dairy products causes vomiting, diarrhea (rapid-onset, 1–6 hours)
  • Skin infections: folliculitis, furunculosis, impetigo from direct contact
  • Toxic shock syndrome: TSST-1 toxin (menstrual and non-menstrual)
  • MRSA: Methicillin-resistant S. aureus — antibiotic-resistant strains in humans and animals (livestock-associated MRSA)
Human infections range from minor skin issues to life-threatening sepsis, endocarditis, pneumonia. Zoonotic transmission from animals to humans occurs via direct contact or food.

📝 COURSE SUMMARY

Key Takeaways from All Lectures

Brucellosis — Key Points (Lec #1)

  • Caused by 5 Brucella species: abortus, melitensis, suis, ovis, canis
  • Gram-negative, non-motile, non-spore-forming coccobacilli
  • Primary disease: reproductive tract infection → abortion in livestock
  • Predilection site: gravid uterus (erythritol in placenta promotes bacterial growth)
  • Transmission: ingestion, coitus, AI, contaminated discharges, milk
  • Cattle abort at 7-8 months; sheep at 3-4 months
  • Diagnosis: culture (serum dextrose agar + CO₂) + serology (SAT, RBPT, MRT, CFT)
  • Control: test & slaughter, vaccination (Strain 19), hygiene
  • Zoonotic: causes undulant fever in humans (via unpasteurized dairy)
  • Biocontainment: BSL-3, Category B bioterrorism agent

Pasteurella haemolytica — Key Points (Lec #2)

  • Gram-negative, non-motile coccobacilli with characteristic bipolar staining (safety-pin appearance)
  • Normal commensal of upper respiratory tract in cattle, sheep, goats
  • Becomes pathogenic following stress: transport, viral infection, overcrowding, poor ventilation
  • Primary disease: shipping fever (fibrinous bronchopneumonia)
  • Primary virulence factor: leukotoxin (destroys alveolar macrophages and neutrophils)
  • Transmission: inhalation of infected droplets from carrier animals
  • Symptoms: high fever, rapid respiration, death within 12-48 hours, spasmodic cough
  • Diagnosis: clinical history (post-transport) + lung lesions + isolation on blood agar
  • Control: vaccination (bacterins/MLV often combined with IBR, PI3, BVD), antibiotics, stress reduction
  • Does NOT grow on MacConkey agar; small translucent colonies on blood agar

Salmonellosis — Key Points (Lec #3)

  • Three main forms: Equine (S. abortus equi, S. typhimurium), Bovine (S. Dublin, S. typhimurium), Avian (Pullorum S. pullorum, Fowl Typhoid S. gallinarum)
  • Horses: S. abortus equi causes abortion; S. typhimurium causes enteritis/abortion; foals get septicemia with 2-3 day mortality
  • Cattle: S. Dublin is host-adapted; S. typhimurium broad-host; carriers shed intermittently; survive 2-4 months in environment
  • Cattle symptoms: fever, diarrhea (may become dysentery), drop in milk, abortion, emaciation; predisposed by parasites, stress, poor nutrition
  • Poultry: Pullorum affects chicks (95% mortality, white diarrhea, huddling); Fowl Typhoid affects adults (green diarrhea, purple comb/wattles)
  • General characteristics: Gram-negative rods; H2S production; non-lactose fermenter; non-motile: S. pullorum, S. gallinarum only
  • Culture: selective media (XLD, Hektoen, SS agar); black-center colonies (H2S+)
  • Control: test-and-cull, biosecurity, vaccination (poultry live attenuated, cattle for Dublin), rodent control
  • Zoonotic: S. typhimurium causes foodborne gastroenteritis via contaminated eggs, poultry, meat, milk

Listeriosis — Key Points (Lec #4)

  • Caused by Listeria monocytogenes only pathogenic species; worldwide distribution in soil, water, silage, decaying vegetation
  • Gram-positive (variable), motile rod (tumbling motility at 22-25°C), non-spore-forming
  • Psychrotrophic: grows at 4°C — concern for refrigerated ready-to-eat foods
  • Primary virulence factor: Listeriolysin O (LLO) — escapes phagosome to cytoplasm; actin-based motility
  • Predilection sites: brain (encephalitis), gravid uterus (abortion), liver (hepatic necrosis), spleen
  • Three clinical forms: Encephalitis (circling, head tilt, jaw paralysis, tongue protrusion, salivation), Abortion (4-8 months), Septicemia (neonates)
  • Characteristic circling to one side (pathognomonic); higher incidence in winter months (linked to silage feeding)
  • Source: infected silage (poorly fermented, moldy); also soil, water; cattle ticks possible vector
  • Diagnosis: clinical signs (circling), brain histopathology (microabscesses), isolation on blood agar (beta-hemolysis, grey colonies), tumbling motility test
  • Control: immediate slaughter of affected animals, destroy carcass (burn/bury), thorough disinfection, avoid spoiled silage
  • Treatment: penicillin G or ampicillin (but generally not recommended for food animals)
  • Zoonotic: foodborne listeriosis from contaminated cheese, deli meats, raw vegetables; high mortality 20-30% in invasive human cases

Escherichia coli — Key Points (Lec #5)

  • Family: Enterobacteriaceae; discovered by Theodor Escherich (1885); normal intestinal flora but pathogenic strains exist
  • Gram-negative, non-spore-forming rods; most strains motile (peritrichous flagella); some capsulated (mucoid colonies)
  • Cultural characteristics: Smooth, moist colonies on NA; metallic green sheen on EMB; pink on MacConkey (lactose fermenter)
  • Biochemical (IMViC): ++-- (Indole +, MR +, VP -, Citrate -); H₂S negative; produces acid + gas from glucose/lactose
  • Antigens: O (somatic, 146 types), H (flagellar, 49 types), K (capsular, 91 types), fimbrial; produces enterotoxins, Shiga toxin (EHEC), colicins
  • Pathogenic strains: ETEC (enterotoxins — neonatal diarrhea), EPEC (attaching/effacing), EHEC (Shiga toxin — HUS, O157:H7), EIEC (invasive), UPEC (UTI)
  • In foals: "Joint Ill" / "Sleepy Foal Disease" — septicemic, fever, diarrhea, high mortality
  • In calves: Colibacillosis — "White Scour" (grayish-white fetid diarrhea); importance of colostrum within 48 hours for passive immunity
  • Diagnosis: Isolation from organs/feces on selective media; IMViC ++--; metallic green sheen on EMB; lactose fermenter
  • Control: immediate colostrum feeding, hygiene (clean calving pens), vaccines for dams/calves, antibiotics (tetracycline, streptomycin — watch resistance)
  • Zoonotic: EHEC O157:H7 causes hemorrhagic colitis and HUS; transmitted via undercooked beef, raw milk, contaminated produce; high public health concern

Streptococcus spp. — Key Points (Lec #6)

  • Gram-positive, spherical cocci in pairs or chains; non-motile, non-spore-forming; normal flora of skin/mucous membranes
  • Hemolysis types: β (complete — clear zone, pathogenic), α (partial — green, S. viridans), γ (none)
  • Lancefield classification: Groups A–V based on C-carbohydrate antigens; important groups: A (S. pyogenes), B (S. agalactiae), C (S. dysgalactiae/S. equi)
  • Virulence factors: Streptolysins (O & S — hemolytic, tissue damaging), streptokinase (fibrinolysin), streptodornase (DNase), hyaluronidase (spreading factor), M-protein (anti-phagocytic)
  • Bovine mastitis: S. agalactiae (chronic, flakes → thick purulent milk), S. uberis (milder, yellow purulent), S. dysgalactiae, S. zooepidemicus
  • Mastitis transmission: calves, milk, hands, utensils; reservoirs: skin (belly/lips), tonsils, rumen, feces
  • Strangles (horses): S. equi — fever, mucopurulent nasal discharge, lymph node abscesses (submaxillary, retropharyngeal), can spread to lungs/liver (bastard strangles)
  • Diagnosis: β-haemolysis on blood agar; CAMP test (+ for S. agalactiae); Edwards medium (Group D); Lancefield serogrouping; PCR/ELISA for S. equi
  • Control: Penicillin (drug of choice); hygiene (milking equipment, teat dipping); culling chronic carriers; strangles — isolation, quarantine, vaccination (variable efficacy)
  • Zoonotic: Rare — S. equi may cause lymphadenitis in humans; S. zooepidemicus opportunistic; dairy workers exposed to mastitis streptococci

Staphylococcus aureus — Key Points (Lec #7)

  • Gram-positive, non-motile cocci in grape-like clusters; facultative anaerobes; normal skin/nares flora but pathogenic strains common
  • Key distinction: coagulase-positive (= S. aureus, pathogenic) vs coagulase-negative (e.g., S. epidermidis, mostly commensal)
  • Cultural: circular, convex, glistening colonies (white to lemon-yellow) on nutrient agar; β-hemolysis on blood agar; grows on MacConkey (pinkish-yellow to red)
  • Biochemical: Gelatin liquefaction (+), grows in 6.5–10% NaCl, ferments glucose/lactose/maltose/sucrose/mannitol with acid (NO gas), MR (+), VP (variable), Indole (−), H₂S (−)
  • Major toxins/enzymes: Coagulase (fibrin shield), Hyaluronidase (spreading), Phosphatase (diagnostic), Fibrinolysin, Hemolysins, Leukocidin, Enterotoxin (food poisoning), Necrotoxin, Lethal toxin
  • Bovine mastitis: Major pathogen — acute (fever, swollen hard udder, blood-tinged → purulent milk, toxemia) and chronic (induration, clots in milk, reduced yield)
  • Reservoirs: skin (udder/teats), nares, feces, bedding, milking equipment; transmission via teat canal; carrier state common (subclinical)
  • Other diseases: Tick pyaemia in lambs, yolk-sac infection/arthritis in poultry, wound abscesses, metritis
  • Diagnosis: β-hemolysis on blood agar; Gram stain (clusters); coagulase test (slide/tube); phosphatase test (phenolphthalein diphosphate agar); tellurite agar selective
  • Control: Penicillin (but resistance common); comprehensive mastitis program — teat dipping, dry cow therapy, culling chronic cases, SCC monitoring, hygiene
  • Vaccines: Available but partial protection only; not fully protective due to strain variation
  • Zoonotic: Major human pathogen — food poisoning (enterotoxin), skin infections, toxic shock syndrome (TSST-1), MRSA (antibiotic-resistant); transmitted via food or direct contact
🎯 Learning Objective: Student should be able to describe morphology, Gram stain, cultural characteristics, coagulase test significance, major virulence factors (toxins/enzymes), clinical presentation of staphylococcal mastitis (acute vs chronic), diagnosis, control measures (antibiotics, hygiene, dry cow therapy, culling), and public health importance (food poisoning, MRSA) of Staphylococcus aureus.
